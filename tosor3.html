The Other Side of Rain
Chapter Three

The Truth Behind the Smile

🌧️

The next day, it didn't rain.
But Minato waited anyway.
He stood near the vending machines, umbrella in hand even though the sky was clear.
And when Rika finally showed up — ten minutes late, hoodie up, eyes hidden — she blinked.
"You're here?"
He simply nodded.
She looked at the umbrella in his hand. "There's no rain."
"I know."
She smiled — the real kind this time. The tired kind.

They didn't walk the usual route. Instead, she led him the long way home, past narrow alleys and quiet streets lined with rusted fences and old laundry.
Eventually, they reached a park. Empty.
Rika sat on the swings, letting her feet drag in the dirt.
Minato stood nearby, watching her carefully.
"This is where I used to go after my parents fought," she said, voice even.
"I'd swing until I couldn't hear them anymore."
Minato didn't interrupt.

"People always think I'm strong," she continued. "Because I laugh. Because I act like I don't care. But sometimes…"
Her voice cracked.
"I wish someone would've asked why I forget my umbrella."
He stepped closer. "Why do you?"
She looked at him.
"I don't know," she whispered. "Maybe because if I get soaked, someone might notice."

· · ·

Silence.
Minato sat on the swing next to hers, hands gripping the chain.
He didn't say "I'm sorry."
He didn't say "It'll get better."
Instead, he pulled something from his pocket.

A small thing, carefully kept
A folded piece of paper. A small, slightly wrinkled crane.
"I saw a girl keep one in her bag once," Minato said softly. "She looked like she was trying to remember something important."

Rika stared at the crane. Then back at him.
"You're weird," she murmured.
"I know."
"…But thank you."
She took the paper crane in both hands, like it was made of glass.
Then she looked up at the empty sky.
"I think I want it to rain tomorrow," she said.
And Minato just replied:
"Me too."

The Rain Returns

The rain came back the next morning.
Not heavy. Not loud. Just a soft drizzle that brushed gently against the windows.
Rika stood by the gates, arms crossed, hoodie up. But no umbrella.
Minato arrived seconds later — not running, not surprised. Just there.
Like he always would be.
Wordlessly, he opened the umbrella.
Rika stepped under it like she belonged there.

They didn't speak for the first few blocks. The sound of rain on the umbrella was enough.
Then she asked, "Did you fold that crane yourself?"
"No," he admitted. "I found it."
"From the girl with the boy who always waits by the cherry tree?"
Minato glanced at her. "You noticed them too," he said.
She nodded. "Hard not to. They look like they're made of the same kind of quiet."
Minato smiled, just slightly. "You and I… we're made of noise we never say out loud."
Rika laughed — the real kind. The one that doesn't need to be loud to feel true.

· · ·

Then she reached into her pocket.
Pulled out the paper crane.
It was dry. Perfectly kept. She'd flattened it, protected it in a small plastic sleeve.
"I'm keeping this," she said. "Like a reminder. That someone saw me."

They stopped at the corner — the same spot where they always parted ways.
Usually, that's where they'd say goodbye.
But today… Rika didn't move.
"Hey," she said. "If it rains again tomorrow…"
Minato tilted his head. "What?"
"Wait for me. Not here. Behind the school. The stairwell. The one no one uses."
"Why?"
Her eyes softened. Because for once, she didn't want to walk with him where the world could see. She wanted to let her heart speak — and it was still learning how.
"…Because I want to try something."
Minato nodded.
"Okay."
