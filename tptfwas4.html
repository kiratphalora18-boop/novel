The Petal That Falls With a Smile
Chapter Four

The Truth She Hid

🌸

It happened on a Tuesday.
The kind of cold that seeped into your bones. The school's heaters barely worked, and most students rushed home right after class.
But Haruki waited. Under the sakura tree.
Two cans of warm coffee in his hands.
One for him. One for her.
Their usual.
She didn't show up.
Not that day.
Not the next.
Not the one after that.
No texts. No calls. Nothing.
He tried not to panic. Maybe she was sick. Maybe her phone broke. Maybe… anything but the feeling gnawing in his chest.
By Friday, he snapped.

He went to the faculty office and asked.
The teacher gave him a strange look.
"She transferred. Didn't she tell you?"
His heart stopped.
"Transferred?"
"Yeah. Back up north. Something about her mother being sick. The paperwork was done before the holidays."
Haruki barely heard the rest.
He ran.
Not home. Not to class. Just… somewhere.
Anywhere.
How could she leave without a word?
After all of it — the rain, the scarf, the almosts, the "not a yes but not a no"?

He ended up at the place where it all started.
The old sakura tree.
And there, taped quietly to the back of the bench, was an envelope. His name on the front.
His hands trembled as he tore it open.
Inside was a single sheet. No flowery paragraphs. Just one sentence.
"I wanted to stay."
And beneath that, written faintly in the corner like a breath she couldn't take—
But I didn't want you to wait for someone still in love with someone else.
Haruki didn't cry.
He just sat there.
Alone.
Two cans of coffee growing cold in the winter wind.

🌸   ·   🌸

The sakura tree bloomed early that year.
Petals danced in the wind like soft, falling promises. Students milled around the courtyard in their pressed uniforms, laughing, taking photos, scribbling their names on each other's yearbooks.
Haruki stood by the tree.
But all he could think about was the past.
He sat on the old bench, ran his fingers along the wood where her envelope had once been taped.
It was smooth now. Faded. Time had done its thing.
"You're late," said a voice behind him.
He turned, startled — but it wasn't her.
Just one of his classmates, smiling awkwardly.
"Everyone's taking a group photo. You coming?"
Haruki shook his head. "In a minute."
They left him alone.
He leaned back and looked up.
The sky was too blue. The kind of blue that made everything hurt more.

From his blazer pocket, he pulled out a letter he'd written weeks ago. A letter that had no destination.
He read it again, lips moving without sound.

A letter with no destination

Hey, Sora.

You were right. I shouldn't have waited.
But I still did.

Not because I hoped you'd come back. But because I wanted you to know—
Someone stayed. Even when you couldn't.
And that mattered. It still does.

He folded it neatly, slid it between the pages of the book she once left on the bench — the one with no cover, still hidden under the loose floorboard they'd found together.
Then he stood.
One look at the tree.
And then Haruki walked away.
As his new term begins without her.
