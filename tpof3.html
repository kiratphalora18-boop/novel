A rice ball, a terrible sandwich, and a quiet pact.

Romance Slice of Life By AKA

Monday started wrong.

Hiro realized it the moment he reached into his bag and felt… nothing.
No bento box. No sandwich. Not even an energy bar. Just his notebook and an eraser that had somehow melted into the bottom corner of his bag like gum.

He sighed.

The bell rang for lunch. His friends ran off toward the cafeteria, yelling something about curry bread.
Hiro didn't follow.
He just sat there, chin resting on his hand, stomach rumbling a little louder with each passing second.

Across the room, Mei was flipping through her sketchbook again, earphones in, eyes calm as always.

Then she looked up.
Their eyes met. It only lasted a second.

But then, she stood.
And walked over.

🥪 His sandwich

"If it was bad, I'd tell you."

12:20 PM · Every lunch

Without saying anything, Mei slid something across his desk — a rice ball. Wrapped in cling film, slightly warm.

Hiro blinked. "Wait, are you—?"

"You'll pass out by math period if you don't eat," she said, not quite meeting his eyes.
"…Are you sure?"
"It's plain rice. Nothing special." Her voice was quiet, but her fingers lingered on the plastic wrap for a second longer than necessary. "I made extras."

He took it gently. "Thanks."

She turned to walk back but paused halfway. Glanced over her shoulder.
"…Next time, bring extra," she said. "So I can try yours too."

Hiro smiled, wide.
"You got it."

· · ·

The next day, he made a sandwich.
It was… questionable. Too much mayonnaise, not enough salt, and the lettuce was a little wilted.

At lunch, he walked over to her desk. Held it out without a word.
She took it. Unwrapped it. Then paused.

"…Is this tuna and… apple slices?"
"…I panicked," he admitted.

She stared at it. Then took a bite.
Hiro held his breath.

"…It's weird," Mei said finally. "But… kinda good."
"You're just being nice."
"No," she said, looking up at him. "If it was bad, I'd tell you."

Something warm passed between them. Like a quiet pact, sealed with mayonnaise and trust.

🔄 From then on
  • ●Rice balls for sandwiches.
  • ●Tamagoyaki for sweet corn.
  • ●Doodled napkin notes exchanged like tiny secrets.
  • ●No one else noticed.

· · ·

School festivals were chaos.

Paper lanterns hung across the courtyard. Students shouted over each other, running around in mismatched costumes, face paint, cat ears, or some combination of all three.

Hiro wasn't really into crowds.
But he wandered anyway, sipping lukewarm soda and chewing on a stick of yakitori that had gone cold fifteen minutes ago.

He hadn't seen Mei all day.
Not at the art club's gallery, not near Class 3-B's haunted house, not even at the goldfish scooping stall where he half-expected her to be sketching behind the scenes.

He almost gave up.

Then he looked up — and saw her.

On the rooftop.
Sitting alone near the railing, feet drawn up to her chest, sketchbook open in her lap. The lights from the courtyard reflected faintly in her eyes.

She didn't look surprised when he opened the rooftop door.
"…Hey," he said, stepping out.
"…Hey," she echoed. "You're late."
"For what?"
She patted the spot beside her.

He sat down.

There was no music up here. No shouting. Just the breeze. The hum of distant laughter. The sky.

She didn't speak for a while. Just kept drawing. Then, without looking up, she said:
"I like being above the noise."
He nodded. "Me too."

🎆 Festival fireworks

Gold and red — reflected in her eyes.

Then a loud boom cracked the sky.
Fireworks.

They shot upward from the back field — bright bursts of gold and red that reflected in her eyes and glowed across her face.

Mei didn't flinch. She just stared, completely still.
Hiro watched the fireworks, then looked at her.

She looked… peaceful.

Then, slowly, without thinking too hard, he shifted his hand closer.

🤏 That moment

Their pinkies brushed.
She didn't move away.

Just whispered, "I thought you hated fireworks."
"I do," he said.
"…Then why are you here?"

He turned to face her, half-laughing.

"Because you're here."

Her eyes flickered toward him — wide for a moment, then soft.
A firework exploded overhead, casting light on her smile.

She didn't say anything else.
But she didn't pull her hand away either.
