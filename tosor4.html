The Other Side of Rain
Chapter Four

What the Rain Was Hiding

🌧️

It rained harder the next day.
The kind of rain that blurred the windows and made the whole school feel quieter than usual.
Students huddled beneath overhangs. PE got cancelled. Teachers rushed through hallways with umbrellas half-opened indoors.
Minato slipped away during the last period.
He didn't ask for permission. He just left.
And waited.

Behind the school. By the old stairwell.
The place where he first found her crying.
Where she dropped her smile and let herself just be.
At first, all he heard was rain.
Then — footsteps.
Slow. Careful.
Rika turned the corner, hair damp, breathing soft.
She didn't say anything at first. Just stood there, watching him like she was waiting to see if he'd disappear.
He didn't.

She stepped forward.
He opened the umbrella.
She didn't take it.
Instead, she stepped under it with him — close.
Closer than before.
Shoulder brushing his.
Heart beating fast.

· · ·

"I don't think I've ever liked someone quietly before," she said.
Minato froze.

A quiet confession
Rika looked up at him, eyes more vulnerable than they had ever been — in the classroom, in the hallways, in the laughs she handed to everyone else.
"I don't even know what I'm doing," she whispered. "But I know it hurts less when you're around."

He swallowed.
"I'm not good with feelings."
"That's okay," she said. "You're good with umbrellas."
He didn't smile. He just held the umbrella steady — rain tapping above them like soft applause.

And then, without thinking, Rika reached for his hand.
Not tightly. Not dramatically.
Just… slipped her fingers between his.
Like it had always been supposed to be that way.
Minato looked down at their hands. Then at her. Then up at the rain.
And whispered:
"I don't want this to be just rain anymore."

Where the Rain Ends and Something Else Begins
Later that afternoon
· · ·

They didn't go home right away.
The rain softened. The clouds broke.
But Rika and Minato stayed there — behind the school, hands still lightly intertwined, umbrella forgotten between them.
Not quite together. Not quite apart.

Rika leaned her head against his shoulder.
"I used to think," she said quietly, "that if I disappeared, no one would really notice. Not me — the real me. Just the version I let them see."
Minato didn't speak. He let her finish. He always did.
"But you…" she breathed. "You noticed things I didn't even know were visible."

The sky above them began to clear.
Light peeked through, soft and gold.
The last of the rain shimmered on the leaves.
Minato turned to her.
"I used to think no one could hear the things I didn't say," he replied.
"But you listened anyway."
There was no kiss. No grand confession.
Just fingers still held together, and eyes that finally looked without looking away.

· · ·

Later, at the front gate, they parted like they always did.
But this time, Rika paused.
She held out something in her hand — something small and familiar.

Her first crane
A folded paper crane.
Minato blinked. "Is that—?"
"I made it this time," she said. "My first one."
He reached out slowly and took it.
It was clumsy. Imperfect. Slightly off-centered.
But it was hers. And it was real.

"Even if it doesn't rain tomorrow," she said, stepping back, "I'll still wait."
And with that, she turned and walked away — hoodie up, footsteps light.
Minato looked down at the crane in his hand.
And for the first time in a long time,
he didn't want the rain to stop.
