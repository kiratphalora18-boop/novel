The day she looked up — just once.

🖼 The Art Festival "The Sound of Dying Stars" — framed, titled, hung.

It was the art festival.

The one day the school walls weren't beige and boring — where every corridor was lined with canvases, photographs, sculptures, all labeled with names and quotes students had chosen to express themselves.

Ren wasn't going to submit anything.

But when he passed by the gallery hallway, he stopped.

There it was — a photo he had taken weeks ago.
The Sound of Dying Stars.
Framed. Printed. Titled in his handwriting… but he hadn't entered it.

He turned the corner and found her.
Sayo stood near the wall, pretending to admire the art.
But she wasn't looking at anything.

"Why'd you do that?" he asked.

She shrugged, not meeting his eyes.
"You take pictures like you're afraid they'll disappear," she said softly.
"People should see them… before they do."

· · ·

Later, on the rooftop, Sayo did something she'd never done before.

She looked up.

Just for a moment — and only with her eyes. Not her heart.

Then she handed him something.

🎀
Her red ribbon. "I won't be wearing this anymore. It's your turn."

"You're giving me a rule-breaker's prize?" he asked.

"No," she said. "I'm giving you a warning."

He looked at her, confused.
But she was already writing in her journal again, back turned, walls rebuilt.

He didn't understand what she meant until the next day.

· · ·

Sayo collapsed during P.E.

Her hands had been shaking. Her knees buckled quietly. No scream, no drama. She just… fell. Like her body had decided it couldn't carry the weight anymore.

The school nurse said it was dehydration.
She didn't return for a week.

During that time, Ren took a photo every day.
But none of them were of the sky.
They were of places she had been.

The rooftop.
Her empty chair.
A drawing left unfinished on the back of her desk.

He's going to ask soon.

· · ·

She came back on a Monday.
Pale, thinner, hair tied in a loose braid — but there.

Ren didn't say anything at first. He just stared at her, quiet relief filling the silence between his heartbeats.

They sat together again at lunch. Rooftop, as always.
Sayo rested her journal beside her, but didn't write.

"Are you okay?" he asked.

She didn't answer.

Instead, she pointed at the clouds drifting above them.
"If you could ask the sky one question," she said, "and get an honest answer… what would you ask?"

Ren looked at her carefully.
Then, without hesitation, he said:

"What's wrong with you, Sayo?"

She froze.

For a moment, it felt like the world stopped spinning.

Her hands trembled — not from cold, not from wind, but from something deeper. Something breaking.

"You said no questions about the journal," she whispered.

"This isn't about the journal," Ren replied.
"This is about you. And I'm scared I'm going to lose you before I even get the chance to know you properly."

Sayo looked down.
Then she reached for her journal — but not to write.
She opened to the first page.
And slowly, shakily, tore it out.

She folded it once. Twice. Handed it to him.
"One answer," she said. "No more. No less."

📄 The torn page

I'm dying, Ren.
And I've known since before I met you.

That's why I made the rules.

He didn't cry.
Not then.

He just sat there, holding that small piece of paper like it weighed more than the sky above them.

She stood up.
"I told you not to fall for me."

He looked up at her, eyes glassy.

"…I didn't fall," he said. "I jumped."

· · ·

That night, he took one photo.
Not of the sky. Not of the school.
But of the torn journal page, taped gently to the wall above his bed.

He asked.

But he stayed.

And now I'm terrified… because I think I want more than the sky can give me.
