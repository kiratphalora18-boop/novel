The Petal That Falls With a Smile
Chapter Three

Confession After the Bell

🌧️

It was raining again. Not heavy, but enough to blur the classroom windows and hush the world outside.
The final bell rang, loud and sharp.
But Haruki didn't move.
His hands were clenched around a crumpled sheet of notes. He wasn't even reading them. His thoughts were spinning too fast, too loud.
Sora hadn't shown up the last two days.
No explanation.
No texts.
No coffee under the sakura tree.
He didn't want to be the clingy guy. The "Are you okay??" kind that people run from.
But it was eating at him. That silence.
So when he saw her walking alone down the empty second-floor corridor—bag in hand, hair slightly damp—he didn't think. He ran.
"Sora!"
She turned, startled. Her eyes widened like she didn't expect anyone to call her name like that.
He slowed down as he reached her.
"…Hey," he said, breathless. "I was... worried."
She blinked. Then offered a quiet, tired smile.
"Sorry. I just... needed some time."
Haruki hesitated.
Then: "From me?"
"No," she said, without missing a beat. "Not from you."
That made his heart ache a little more than it should have.

There were a hundred things he could've said. A hundred jokes he could've made. But all of them felt wrong.
So he said what he meant. All of it.
"I like you."
The hallway went still.
Even the rain outside seemed to pause for a second.
"I don't know when it started," Haruki continued, trying not to look away. "But it's there. I don't want this to be just conversations under a tree. I want to know you... even the parts you're scared to share."
Sora stared at him.
Not shocked.
Not scared.
Just… silent.
For a long moment.
Then, finally, she spoke.
"…You shouldn't," she whispered.
He flinched. "Why not?"
"Because I can't be what you want," she said, eyes glistening. "Because I'm still not over what I broke. And because… I might break you too."
Haruki stepped forward.
"Let me decide that."
Another silence. Another inhale she held too long.
Then Sora nodded — just slightly.
"I'm not saying yes," she said. "But I'm not walking away either."
And that was enough.
They stood there for a while, rain tapping gently on the windows behind them.
Two high schoolers, hearts fragile, on the edge of something they didn't yet understand.

🌸   ·   🌸

The winter break passed like a soft dream.
No dramatic kisses. No fireworks. Just quiet mornings. Occasional texts.
And on the first day back, Haruki found her waiting under the sakura tree — scarf wrapped tight, cheeks pink from the cold.
"Hey," she said, looking up as he arrived.
Haruki grinned. "Wow. I didn't imagine you. You actually exist outside my head."
"I debated showing up," she muttered. "Figured you might've moved on."
He nudged her shoulder. "You underestimate how annoying I can be."
She smiled.
And just like that, they slipped into their rhythm again — but it wasn't exactly the same. Something had shifted.
They sat closer now. Their hands brushed more often.
And though she never said yes, she didn't say no either.
It was… almost happiness.

But Haruki noticed the way Sora would stare off sometimes, eyes fixed on a place that wasn't there.
How she'd laugh but then immediately fall silent, like she remembered something she shouldn't have.
One afternoon, as they walked home together beneath a sky the color of faded denim, Haruki asked quietly,
"Do you ever think about the past?"
She stopped walking.
He turned. "Sorry. Was that—"
"I do," she interrupted. "All the time."
He waited.
Then she added, "There's a boy. Back then. He was warm. And kind. I… ruined that."
Haruki swallowed. "Did he love you?"
"I think so."
"And you?"
She looked away. "I still do."

Those words landed like a stone in his chest. But he didn't flinch.
Because the truth was — he knew.
He had always known that he was reaching for someone still mourning a ghost.
Still holding the echoes of a different heartbeat.
But love isn't always rational.
"…I'm not asking you to forget him," Haruki said. "Just… let me stay. While you remember."
For a moment, she looked at him like he was the saddest thing she'd ever seen.
Then she nodded. Once.
And they kept walking — under January skies that looked like they could collapse at any moment.

— End of Chapter Three —
