📷 The Day She Stayed · Chapter 4

The Rule We
Broke Together

One sky. Two cameras. No more rules.

Romance Drama Sad By AKA

After that day, they didn't talk about it again.

Not directly.
No hospital names. No diagnoses. No countdowns.
But everything changed.

Ren stopped photographing the sky.

Instead, he started carrying a second camera — an old one, scratched but reliable — and offered it to her.

Sayo blinked. "Why two?"

"One for me. One for you," he said.
"If the sky really matters… maybe I should see it through your eyes too."

She hesitated — then took it.

📷 Ren
+
📸 Sayo

Two cameras. One sky.

That evening, they both stood on the rooftop.

Ren took a picture of the clouds turning orange.
Sayo, silently, turned her lens toward him.

Click.

"Rule Two," she whispered, smiling just a little.
"Officially broken."

He looked at her, surprised. "That was a picture of me?"

She nodded. Then added, "And no, you don't get to delete it."

· · ·

The days that followed were different.

✨ The days that followed
  • ●They skipped class once — just once — to chase a coastline she'd never seen.
  • ●They argued about music, laughed over ice cream, shared headphones.
  • ●Watched dogs on the street.
  • ●And once… just once… held hands without saying anything about it.

She wore the red ribbon again that day — in her hair, loose and slightly faded.

Later that night, Ren found a photo left on his desk.
Printed. Untitled.
It was of him — looking up at the sky with his camera, wind blowing through his hair, completely unaware.

Sayo had captured it.

On the back, in her handwriting:

✍️ Written on the back

"This is how I'll remember you —
before the world forgets."

🔒 Ren's private folder That night.

We've broken Rule 1 and 2.
Which leaves only one left.

And I think I broke it a long time ago.

Sayo, I'm in love with you.
I just don't know if I'm allowed to say it yet.

· · ·

The days grew softer.
Not brighter — just softer.
Like the world knew what was coming, but was too kind to say it out loud.

Sayo started wearing her ribbon more often. Not in her hair, but around her wrist — tied loosely, like a reminder she wasn't ready to forget yet.

Ren, meanwhile, kept taking photos. But now his lens was almost always turned toward her — in shadows, in laughter, in moments she didn't know she was being seen.

She stopped stopping him.

· · ·

On the last Friday of the month, their class was assigned a project:
"Capture something you'll never forget."

The whole class groaned.
Ren and Sayo didn't.

They sat in silence after school, side by side on the rooftop, the golden hour casting halos on their shoulders.

"You already know what you're going to do?" she asked him.

"I already did it."

She tilted her head. "When?"

He looked at her. Carefully. Softly.

"Every time I looked at you."

Sayo laughed. But it was the quiet kind — breathy, fragile, like her ribs were too tired to hold anything heavy.

"Then I guess I should tell you something," she whispered.

Ren turned. "What is it?"

But she didn't answer right away.

Instead, she reached into her journal. Tore out another page. Folded it once. Then placed it into his palm.

"Don't open it now," she said. "Open it… only when you know."

"Know what?"

She looked at him — really looked — and for the first time, her eyes weren't sad.
They were full.
Full of everything she never said.

"…That I broke the last rule too."

· · ·

That night, Ren didn't sleep.

He held the letter. Didn't open it.

And wondered if maybe knowing would make it real.
