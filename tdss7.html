🎀 The Day She Stayed · Chapter 7

Rewriting the Ending

She didn't just survive. She chose to keep living.

Romance Drama Sad By AKA

The school was loud.

Louder than she remembered. Hallways full of footsteps, laughter, forgotten projects and snacks left in lockers. The same chaos… but it felt new now. Like she was walking through it for the first time — not as a ghost, not as someone counting days…

But as Sayo. Just Sayo.

She paused at the classroom door. Ren stood beside her, holding her bag like it was made of glass.

"You okay?" he whispered.

She nodded. Then took a deep breath and stepped inside.

The Moment Everything Stopped 🎀

The room froze. Chairs squeaked.
Whispers passed like ripples in a lake.
The girl who disappeared.

People turned. Some gasped. Some stared.

The quiet one. The one with the ribbon and the journal and the boy who never stopped looking at her seat.

And then — the teacher smiled.

"Welcome back, Sayo."

She smiled back.

"Sorry I was gone so long. I was… rewriting my ending."

She took her seat — the same one beside Ren, by the window. The ribbon was tied around her wrist again. Faded. Beautiful.

Ren leaned in, whispering: "You stole the spotlight."

She smirked. "I earned it."

— A Sky Worth Sharing

It wasn't some fancy place. No lights, no music, no crowd. Just a train ride. A hill. A picnic basket. And two people who once thought they'd never have this kind of moment.

Their first official date.

Sayo laid out the blanket beneath a tree, the city lights distant behind them and the horizon melting into orange. Ren set down his camera gently, like he was afraid it might interrupt the silence.

She watched him from across the blanket.

"You look like you're about to propose," she teased.

Ren choked on his sandwich. "I— I mean… not yet."

Sayo laughed — soft, real. A sound he would've crossed lifetimes to hear again.

🧺
A Hill. A Picnic Basket. A Horizon in Orange. Their First Official Date · No fancy place needed

They didn't talk much. Just exchanged glances, gestures, shared bites of food and a soda with too little fizz. But it felt full. Full of the space between two people who had seen the edge — and still reached for each other.

Later, he picked up his camera.

"Can I?" he asked.

She nodded, brushing her hair back. He took the shot — no filters, no angles, no tricks. Just her in golden light, smiling toward him like she wasn't scared anymore.

He looked at the preview screen. Then at her.

"…This one," he whispered, "is my favorite."

She walked over, peeking at the photo.

"It's not the sky."

"No," he said. "It's who I look up to now."

And then, with the sun dipping behind them and the breeze gentle on her skin — Sayo leaned in. Not fast. Not dramatic. Just enough.

Their foreheads touched. Her eyes closed.

And in a voice barely heard:

"I love you, Ren."

He didn't rush to reply. He took her hand, gently placed it over his heart, and said:

"You don't have to be dying to hear that back."

Then he kissed her. Slow. Honest. Full of all the days they thought they'd never have.
